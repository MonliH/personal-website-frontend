> In 1934, David Hilbert was sitting next to the Nazis' newly appointed minister of education, at a banquet. When asked, "And how is mathematics 
> in Göttingen, now that it has been freed of the Jewish influence?" Hilbert replied: "Mathematics in Göttingen, there is really no such thing any more."
>
> -- [Historical Encyclopedia of Natural and Mathematical Sciences, pg. 4540](https://books.google.de/books?id=9tUrarQYhKMC&pg=PA4540&lpg=PA4540&dq=gottingen+purge+1933&source=bl&ots=24AvXWdFdF&sig=UjmT-_RO-lIBh40mRHaZO12Pkwc&hl=en&sa=X&ei=sYVVVPvoLcH4OuSsgKgH&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q=gottingen%20purge%201933&f=false)

Göttingen university was at a time the premier place to work on quantum theory, housing the likes of Bohr, Heisenberg, and Born. After the rise
of Nazi Germany, the purging of some of it's most talented researchers decisively led to it's decline.

The university as an institution has proven remarkably durable. But any given university's greatness has not: Göttingen shows how fast it evaporates 
once the people leave.

Good universities are known to house the brightest students and academics. This collection of bright students persists over time, as prospective students 
are attracted by the prestige and opportunities when associating with a good university. Some good universities also have more funding than most Fortune 
500 companies. However, their funding is only a second-order contributor to their influence. The first-order contributor is the students - money and 
influence follows from impactful people.

The success of today's modern AI research labs hinges on the same idea. With Anthropic and OpenAI reaching a private valuation of almost
$1 trillion each, it's easy to forget that they are doing *research* - i.e., trying to solve the hardest problems in AI that *nobody knows how to solve*.
They are not particularly advantaged in any way except in attracting talent, in the same way that universities are. They do have vastly more funding
than other AI labs, but again, that is only a second-order contributor to their success. A more talented team will accrue funding at a faster rate than
a less talented team.

Unlike traditional startups, there are no products or assets that provide an advantage in their mission of building recursively self-improving 
AI in the long-term, except human talent. Other factors, such as existing products, compute access, or funding help shape narrative, which helps 
to attract talent.

But it will be *people* who build recursively self-improving AI. I would like to reiterate that nobody knows how to build that right now, 
nor is there any (even weak) guarantee that "frontier" modeling labs are taking the correct path to get there. The fact that frontier labs are 
ahead on benchmarks, compute, and funding only helps these labs mask their due intellectual humility - that nobody knows how to make 
recursively self-improving AI.

Before we figure that out, the north star of all AI labs should be to retain smart people, to house them and build up their careers, as colleges do.
